## Pricing

At $155 per month without insurance or $105 per month with insurance, Low T Center is one of the few telehealth-adjacent TRT providers that actually accepts insurance - and the pricing reflects it. The $50-per-month gap adds up to $600 per year if your plan qualifies, so it's worth checking before you assume you'll pay the higher rate.

Both tiers include the same package: custom prefilled syringes, home delivery, and remote monitoring. The initial testosterone test is genuinely free, but the second qualifying visit before treatment begins may carry its own fee - that's worth asking about before you book.

FSA and HSA eligibility isn't confirmed publicly, so check with your benefits administrator if that matters to your budget.

## What's included

Both pricing tiers cover monthly delivery of custom prefilled testosterone syringes, remote monitoring by the clinical team, and ongoing provider optimization of your protocol. In-house labs at clinic locations handle the initial bloodwork, and labs are included in ongoing care - you're not paying separately for each blood draw.

The prefilled syringe format is a practical detail worth noting. Standard TRT typically involves drawing testosterone from a multi-dose vial with a large-gauge needle, then swapping to a finer needle for injection. Prefilled syringes skip that step and remove a common source of dosing error for people new to self-injection.

## Pros and cons

The strongest case for Low T Center is the combination of longevity and insurance acceptance. Being in business since 2009 means they've navigated regulatory shifts, supply chain problems, and the general volatility of the telehealth TRT space - that's a meaningful track record when many newer entrants have come and gone. Accepting insurance is also genuinely unusual in this market, and it moves the needle on affordability for patients with qualifying plans.

The physical clinic network is both an asset and a limitation. Forty-seven locations with in-house labs is valuable if you're within driving distance; it's an immediate dealbreaker if you're not. The requirement for two in-person visits before treatment begins also makes this a worse fit for patients who specifically want a fully remote experience.

The injection-only approach is worth flagging upfront. Some men can't or prefer not to self-inject, and Low T Center offers no gel or cream alternative. If you're uncertain about injections, this probably isn't the right starting point.

Geographic coverage is the hardest constraint. Eleven states sounds substantial until you check the list - large swaths of the country, including most of the Southeast beyond Texas and the entire coasts, have no coverage at all.

## Bottom line

Low T Center is a solid option for men in its coverage area who want physician-led care, can tolerate two upfront in-clinic visits, and are comfortable with self-injection. The insurance acceptance and prefilled syringe delivery are genuine differentiators, and 17 years of operation provides the kind of track record most newer telehealth entrants can't offer. If you're outside those 11 states, or you need a fully remote intake, look elsewhere.
