## How Cenegenics works

The process starts with a full-day in-center assessment, typically four to six hours. This is not a video call - it is a physical visit to one of their locations. Before you arrive, a phlebotomist comes to your home to collect baseline blood samples so your lab results are ready on the day of the assessment. At the center, you go through a battery of testing that goes well beyond a standard hormone panel. From there, a dedicated team - physician, health coach, patient coordinator, and phlebotomist - is assigned to your case. Follow-up care uses a combination of in-person visits and telehealth. Medications are shipped directly to you.

Cenegenics is available in 17 states and territories: AK, AZ, CA, CO, DC, FL, GA, IL, NC, NV, NY, OH, OK, PA, PR, TX, and UT.

## Pricing

Public pricing isn't disclosed; you'll get a quote after the intake. Given the scope of the initial evaluation - 270-plus biomarkers, VO2 max testing, DEXA body composition scanning, cardiac assessment, and cognitive testing, plus an ongoing multidisciplinary team - this is not a budget program. Plan to ask directly what the program costs before you commit time to the intake process. Whether FSA or HSA funds apply and whether any insurance is accepted are not confirmed in public materials.

## What's included

The diagnostic scope is the most obvious differentiator. Most telehealth TRT platforms run a handful of markers - total testosterone, free testosterone, estradiol, PSA, hematocrit - and call it comprehensive. Cenegenics' baseline assessment covers 270-plus biomarkers alongside VO2 max, DEXA, cardiac, and cognitive testing. That combination gives a clinical picture that almost no other TRT-adjacent program attempts.

The team structure is also meaningfully different from the single-physician telehealth model. You have a physician who knows your case, a health coach for accountability and lifestyle guidance, a coordinator managing your logistics, and a phlebotomist for ongoing blood work. In-home phlebotomy at baseline removes one logistical hurdle.

Testosterone is one possible outcome of the program, not the default starting point. It gets prescribed if the evaluation supports it. If your primary goal is TRT specifically, Cenegenics may or may not align with that - the program is built around optimization broadly, not hormone replacement as a standalone service.

## Pros and cons

The case for Cenegenics starts with diagnostic depth. If you have ever gone through a basic hormone panel and felt like you were getting a partial answer, a 270-plus biomarker workup combined with VO2, DEXA, cardiac, and cognitive assessments is a genuinely different level of baseline data. The 29-year track record in age management medicine is real - Cenegenics has been refining this approach longer than most competitors have existed. The team model means more continuity than you get from a rotating pool of telehealth providers, and in-home phlebotomy is a concrete convenience that most clinics don't offer.

The tradeoffs are also real. The full-day in-center assessment is a serious time commitment, and depending on where you live, it may require travel. Pricing opacity is a recurring frustration in this space, but it stings more here given what you are being asked to invest in terms of time before you know what the program costs. And if you came in specifically wanting testosterone therapy, the program's broader longevity orientation means there is no guarantee that is where you land - Cenegenics prescribes hormones when clinically indicated, not on request.

## Bottom line

Cenegenics makes sense for men who want a rigorous, full-picture health evaluation and are comfortable with a premium, in-person-first program. The diagnostic depth, team model, and nearly three decades of practice in this specialty separate it from the majority of what is on the market. What it is not is a convenient, affordable TRT option - the full-day assessment, undisclosed pricing, and longevity-first approach mean it is best suited to patients for whom testosterone therapy is one part of a bigger picture, not the whole point.
