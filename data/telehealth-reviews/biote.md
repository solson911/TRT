## How Biote works

Because Biote operates through a certified provider network, your first step is finding a Biote-trained provider in your area using the locator on their website. From there, the experience is shaped by that individual practice - not a centralized Biote platform. You schedule with the local provider, get bloodwork done (labs are included as part of the process), and your provider uses those results to determine the right pellet formulation for you.

Pellets are small, compressed cylinders - roughly the size of a grain of rice - made from bioidentical hormones. A provider inserts them under the skin, typically near the hip, in a brief in-office procedure. The pellets release hormones gradually over several months, at which point you return for a follow-up and, if appropriate, another insertion. There's no daily pill, no weekly injection, no topical cream to apply and worry about transferring to a partner or child. You go in, get the procedure done, and leave.

Because the prescribing happens through independent practices that have been trained and certified by Biote, the model is mixed - you're seeing a real local provider, but that provider is following a protocol that Biote has developed and refined.

## Pricing

Public pricing isn't disclosed; you'll get a quote after the intake with your local Biote-certified provider. This makes upfront comparison shopping difficult. Pellet therapy in general tends to run higher than injections or topicals, and costs vary by region and practice. Whether insurance, FSA, or HSA can be applied is up to your individual provider's billing setup; Biote itself doesn't publish guidance on coverage.

## What's included

Labs are part of the process - your bloodwork informs the pellet formulation, so testing isn't an add-on you need to arrange separately. Beyond that, what's included depends heavily on the practice you're working with. Because Biote is a network of independent providers, there's no standardized patient portal, no centralized messaging system, and no Biote-branded care team fielding your questions between visits.

The pellets themselves are not shipped. The procedure has to happen in-office by definition, so this isn't a model built for people who want fully remote care.

## Pros and cons

The strongest argument for Biote is track record. Six million-plus pellet insertions is a meaningful number, and the company has been in the space long enough to develop a well-documented protocol. If you're specifically interested in pellet therapy and want a provider trained on a consistent methodology, the certification network offers some assurance over going to a solo practitioner who recently added pellets to their menu.

Bioidentical hormones are also central to Biote's approach. Bioidentical means the hormone is molecularly identical to what your body produces naturally, as opposed to synthetic analogs - a distinction that matters to some patients and their providers, though it doesn't automatically make pellets the right delivery method for everyone.

The limitations are real, though. Pellet-only means that if you prefer injections, creams, oral medications, or any other format, you're out of luck with Biote. The network model also means the patient experience can vary significantly from one practice to the next - Biote controls the training, not the day-to-day care. And because this is an in-office procedure rather than a mail-order prescription, you need a certified provider within reasonable driving distance. For people in rural areas or states with sparse network coverage, that may not be realistic.

The pricing opacity is a genuine friction point. Without public numbers, you can't easily compare Biote to alternatives before you've already invested time in a consultation.

## Bottom line

Biote is best suited to patients who have already decided they want pellet therapy and want a provider trained on a protocol with documented experience behind it. It's not designed for people who want a fast, fully remote TRT experience, and it's not a good fit if you want flexibility in delivery method. Think of it less as a telehealth company and more as a quality standard for a specific kind of in-office hormone therapy.
