## Pros and cons

The strongest argument for Biote is track record. Six million-plus pellet insertions is a meaningful number, and the company has been in the space long enough to develop a well-documented protocol. If you're specifically interested in pellet therapy and want a provider trained on a consistent methodology, the certification network offers some assurance over going to a solo practitioner who recently added pellets to their menu.

Bioidentical hormones are also central to Biote's approach. Bioidentical means the hormone is molecularly identical to what your body produces naturally, as opposed to synthetic analogs - a distinction that matters to some patients and their providers, though it doesn't automatically make pellets the right delivery method for everyone.

The limitations are real, though. Pellet-only means that if you prefer injections, creams, oral medications, or any other format, you're out of luck with Biote. The network model also means the patient experience can vary significantly from one practice to the next - Biote controls the training, not the day-to-day care. And because this is an in-office procedure rather than a mail-order prescription, you need a certified provider within reasonable driving distance. For people in rural areas or states with sparse network coverage, that may not be realistic.

The pricing opacity is a genuine friction point. Without public numbers, you can't easily compare Biote to alternatives before you've already invested time in a consultation.

## Bottom line

Biote is best suited to patients who have already decided they want pellet therapy and want a provider trained on a protocol with documented experience behind it. It's not designed for people who want a fast, fully remote TRT experience, and it's not a good fit if you want flexibility in delivery method. Think of it less as a telehealth company and more as a quality standard for a specific kind of in-office hormone therapy.
