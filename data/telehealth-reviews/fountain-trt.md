## Pros and cons

The pricing model's biggest strength is its clarity. You know exactly what you're paying - $199 a month, nothing hidden - and follow-up visits don't cost extra. For men who dislike needles or find weekly injections inconvenient, a topical option with no office visits and a physician at the other end of a video call is genuinely useful. The urologist-led clinical structure also stands out; a lot of TRT telehealth services lean on general practitioners or NPs, and Doron Stember's urology background is relevant when testosterone therapy can touch prostate health and fertility concerns.

The limitations are hard to ignore, though. Labs being excluded is a meaningful friction point. Most competing platforms either ship lab kits or cover bloodwork as part of the plan; here you're responsible for sourcing and funding your own draws, indefinitely. For men without good insurance coverage or a cooperative primary care doctor, that adds real cost and hassle. Coverage is capped at 20 states, which rules out a significant portion of the country. And with cream as the only option, anyone who has tried topical testosterone and found poor absorption - which isn't uncommon - or who simply prefers the predictability of injections has nowhere to go within this platform.

## Bottom line

Fountain TRT works well for a specific type of patient: someone in one of its 20 covered states, comfortable with topical application, who wants a physician-only TRT program at a predictable monthly rate and already has a reliable, low-cost way to handle bloodwork. The $199 flat fee is competitive on paper, but the full cost picture shifts once you account for labs. As with any testosterone therapy, you'll need a proper clinical evaluation and up-to-date bloodwork before starting - testosterone is a Schedule III controlled substance, and no responsible provider should skip that step.
