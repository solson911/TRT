A new market-research forecast from DataM Intelligence projects that the global testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) market will reach $2.74 billion by 2033, citing rising hypogonadism diagnoses, an aging male population, and the expansion of telehealth-based prescribing as the main growth drivers. The figure was distributed via a press release rather than published in a peer-reviewed source, so it should be read as one firm's commercial outlook on a category, not a clinical or epidemiological finding.

The forecast names AbbVie, Endo Pharmaceuticals, Besins Healthcare, and Pfizer as the established players shaping supply. AbbVie's AndroGel remains one of the most widely prescribed topical testosterone products in the United States. Endo's Aveed, a long-acting injectable testosterone undecanoate, sits at the other end of the dosing-frequency spectrum and competes for the patient population that prefers fewer in-clinic visits. Besins is a hormone-therapy specialist with a stronger international footprint than U.S. profile, and Pfizer's role is more diversified across men's health rather than concentrated in a single TRT brand.

For TRT patients and the clinicians who treat them, the more useful question isn't whether the market will hit $2.74 billion, but what's actually driving the curve. Three forces show up repeatedly in industry analysis and aren't unique to this report.

The first is the demographic baseline. Hypogonadism prevalence rises with age, and the population of U.S. men over 40 is expanding both in absolute numbers and as a share of the adult population. Even at flat per-capita treatment rates, the eligible pool grows. The second is the diagnostic pipeline: routine total-testosterone testing has become more common in primary care, partly because patients ask for it after seeing direct-to-consumer marketing, and partly because employer-sponsored "executive physicals" and longevity-focused practices include it as standard. More tests produce more diagnoses, and a fraction of those diagnoses convert into long-term prescriptions.

The third, and the one most relevant to the U.S. market, is telehealth. Companies like Hone Health, Marek Health, Hims, Maximus, and others have moved a meaningful share of new TRT prescriptions online. The clinical model varies. Some run their own affiliated providers, some contract with independent clinicians, and some use asynchronous intake that gets reviewed before any prescription is written. The competitive pressure has pushed pricing down, shortened time-to-first-injection, and broadened geographic access for patients who don't live near a men's-health clinic. It has also drawn regulatory attention. State medical boards and the FDA have both signaled growing scrutiny of whether telehealth TRT is being prescribed within the bounds of established hypogonadism guidelines, particularly when patients are placed on therapy after a single morning total-testosterone draw rather than two confirmatory measurements.

The DataM forecast also flags emerging-markets growth and a gradual shift toward long-acting formulations. Both are plausible. Long-acting injectables and pellets reduce dosing frequency and adherence drop-off, which matters for chronic therapies. International growth is harder to handicap because regulatory regimes vary widely, and several countries restrict TRT marketing or impose stricter prescribing pathways than the U.S.

What the forecast doesn't say is also worth noting. Market-size projections aggregate brand-name pharmaceuticals, generic compounded preparations, and increasingly, peptide and SERM-based alternatives that some clinicians prescribe for men who want to preserve fertility on therapy. The line between "the TRT market" and "the broader androgen-optimization market" is fuzzier than it was a decade ago, and forecasts that lump them together can over- or underestimate any one segment.

For now, the practical takeaway for patients is simple: the category is well-funded and getting more crowded, which usually means more options, more pricing pressure, and a steadier supply of the major branded products. It also means more aggressive marketing. Anyone considering TRT should still get a confirmed diagnosis from a licensed clinician, two morning total-testosterone draws, and a workup that rules out reversible causes before starting therapy. A growing market doesn't change the standard of care.
