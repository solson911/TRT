A newly indexed narrative review on PubMed reframes testosterone deficiency in aging males as a dual threat, linking low androgen levels to both coronary artery calcification (CAC) and osteoporosis. Rather than treating cardiovascular and skeletal decline as separate age-related processes, the authors argue that declining testosterone is a shared upstream driver connecting vascular stiffening with accelerating bone loss in elderly men.

The review synthesizes current evidence on how testosterone influences vascular biology, including effects on endothelial function, smooth muscle calcium handling, inflammatory signaling, and lipid metabolism. Men with clinically low total or free testosterone consistently show higher CAC scores on cardiac imaging, and the authors highlight mechanistic pathways by which hypogonadism may accelerate the transition from subclinical atherosclerosis to calcified, higher-risk plaque.

On the skeletal side, the review reinforces testosterone's direct and indirect roles in maintaining bone mineral density. Beyond its well-known conversion to estradiol via aromatase - a major contributor to male bone homeostasis - testosterone supports osteoblast activity, suppresses osteoclast-mediated resorption, and modulates muscle mass that loads bone. The authors describe how deficiency tips this balance toward net resorption, producing the rising fracture risk observed in older hypogonadal men.

The most clinically provocative thread is the overlap between these two conditions. Vascular calcification and bone demineralization share regulators - including RANKL/OPG signaling, matrix Gla protein, and vitamin K- and D-dependent pathways - which may explain why the same patients often present with both problems. The review suggests that low testosterone may act as a common permissive factor, accelerating calcium misplacement from bone into arterial walls.

For clinicians evaluating older men, the authors argue that testosterone status deserves consideration alongside traditional cardiovascular and bone risk factors, and that patients found to have one condition should be screened for the other. They stop short of recommending testosterone replacement therapy as a preventive intervention for CAC or osteoporosis, noting that randomized outcome data remain limited and that current TRT guidelines are built around symptomatic hypogonadism rather than vascular or skeletal endpoints. The review calls for prospective trials designed to test whether restoring testosterone to physiologic ranges meaningfully alters CAC progression and fracture incidence in aging men.
