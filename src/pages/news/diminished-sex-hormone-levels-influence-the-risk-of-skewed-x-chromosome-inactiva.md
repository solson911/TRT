A new preprint posted to medRxiv reports that diminished circulating sex hormone levels are associated with a higher risk of skewed X chromosome inactivation (XCI) in immune cells. The finding adds an endocrine angle to a phenomenon that's usually discussed in purely genetic or aging terms, and it hints at a biological crossover between hormonal status and the cellular composition of the immune system.

X chromosome inactivation is the process that randomly silences one of the two X chromosomes in each cell of a 46,XX female, equalizing X-linked gene expression between females and 46,XY males. Because the choice is random early in development, most tissues end up with a roughly 50/50 mosaic of cells using the maternal versus paternal X. In some women, though, immune cells drift heavily toward one X, a pattern the authors call XCI-skew, defined as 75% or more of cells carrying the same inactivated X.

The authors framed their work around a simple question: if XCI-skew tracks with age and with immune changes, does it also track with the hormonal shifts that accompany reproductive aging? Sex hormones are well known to shape immune cell populations, and menopause brings a steep decline in both estrogens and androgens. That pairing made sex hormone status a logical variable to test against XCI ratios measured in blood.

Using cohort data that paired immune cell XCI measurements with circulating sex hormone assays, the researchers examined whether women with lower hormone levels were more likely to present with skewed ratios. Their analysis points to an association: diminished sex hormone levels were linked to a higher probability of XCI-skew, consistent with the idea that a less hormone-supported immune compartment may be more prone to the clonal drift that produces skewed inactivation patterns over time.

The authors are careful about causality. XCI-skew in blood is typically read as a sign of reduced hematopoietic stem cell pool size, or of selection pressures favoring cells that carry one X over the other. Lower sex hormones could plausibly contribute to either mechanism, but the preprint is an association study, not a controlled experiment. What it establishes is that endocrine status belongs in the conversation when interpreting XCI-skew, alongside chronological age and prior immune exposures.

Why it matters for a TRT-oriented audience: the study is in women, but the underlying theme - that sex hormone levels help shape immune cell dynamics - cuts across sexes. Hypogonadal men also show immune shifts that correlate with testosterone status, and the idea that hormone deficiency can leave measurable fingerprints on the immune compartment reinforces a broader message. Hormones are not only drivers of muscle, libido, and mood; they're part of the background environment that keeps immune cell populations balanced.

There are limits to flag. The preprint has not yet been peer reviewed, the XCI-skew threshold of 75% is a convention rather than a biologically sharp cutoff, and the hormone measurements reflect a single point in time rather than lifetime exposure. Replication in independent cohorts, ideally with longitudinal hormone and XCI data, will be needed before anyone reads clinical implications into the signal.

For now, the practical takeaway is limited. There's no suggestion in the paper that measuring XCI-skew should enter routine workups, and no claim that hormone therapy alters the trajectory of XCI ratios. What the work does is tighten the conceptual link between endocrine aging and immune aging, and give researchers a concrete variable to track as that link gets tested in larger datasets.

As always, decisions about hormone testing or therapy should be made with a licensed clinician who can weigh an individual's full picture rather than extrapolating from a single preprint.
